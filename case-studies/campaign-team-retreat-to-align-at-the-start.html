Campaign Team Retreat to Align at the Start

Kickoff retreat to set norms, routines, and rituals.

Service
Team Coaching

The team as the unit of change

The Partnership

Early in a campaign, momentum matters — but alignment matters more. As Eli Northrup's New York State Assembly campaign transitioned from vision to execution, the core team gathered at a critical juncture. Roles were forming, strategy was coming into focus, and the pace was accelerating. What remained underdeveloped was the shared operating system needed to sustain trust, clarity, and coordinated action through a demanding election cycle. We designed and facilitated a one-day kickoff retreat to help the team align for faster, coordinated movement.

The Challenge

Campaign teams face seemingly endless challenges and tensions, often including urgency without alignment. Eli's team was committed and invested in winning, but navigated an emerging (yet uncodified) path to victory, overlapping roles with unnamed informal power, and the need to build trust quickly while already in motion. Without intentional space to clarify roles, decision-making, and team norms, even strong teams lose energy to confusion, duplication, or unspoken friction.

Our Approach

A one-day Campaign Team Kickoff Retreat focused on clarity, coordination, and care. The day began personally, with team members sharing identified strengths and weaknesses. The team then accomplished three essential goals.

Goal 1 — Anchor to the path to victory

Strategic conversation connected goals, strategies, audiences, messages, tactics, and timing, so everyone understood how their role contributed to winning. Rather than finalizing every detail, the work focused on building a collective orientation to success.

Goal 2 — Make roles clear and power visible

Through role mapping and real campaign scenarios — events, volunteer engagement, communications moments — the team clarified where responsibility begins and ends, how roles intersect under pressure, and how to leverage the candidate's informal group of key advisors, the “Kitchen Cabinet.” This surfaced direct conversation about formal and informal power, reduced ambiguity, and set conditions for coordinated decision-making.

Goal 3 — Establish habits that support performance and sustainability

The team co-created norms, routines, and rituals to guide day-to-day work: clear communication and feedback expectations, weekly rhythms for data review and goal-setting, and rituals attending to morale, connection, and meaning. The emphasis was on building a campaign culture that could endure — not just efficiency.

The Results

By the retreat's end, the campaign team had a shared understanding of the path to victory, clearer role definitions and interdependencies, explicit norms and routines supporting accountability and care, and momentum grounded in alignment rather than urgency alone.

The team developed shared language for how they wanted to show up for the campaign and for each other. They named and addressed power dynamics and decision-making structures, and committed to give and receive real-time feedback, ask for help early, and lead through ambiguity. Leadership became understood not as titles or functions, but as daily practices for decision-making, responsibility-taking, and caring for each other on the path to winning.
