Repairing a Ruptured Relationship

2:2 coaching to repair a manager-employee relationship.

Service
Team Coaching

The team as the unit of change

The Partnership

An organization engaged us to support a manager and their direct report whose working relationship had become strained and unproductive. Both individuals were deeply committed to the mission and highly capable in their roles, but tension, misalignment, and mistrust were beginning to undermine performance. Team health suffered.

Key Takeaway2x2 coaching turned a cycle of defensiveness and mistrust between a manager and their report into clearer expectations, safer feedback, and restored good faith.

We engaged them in 2x2 Coaching -a structured, relational approach informed by our training in the Cape Cod Model, which emphasizes awareness, a sense of possibility, and choice. This work is designed to strengthen individual leadership while working to repair and reinforce the relationship between two people.

The Challenge

The manager–employee relationship was marked by a cycle of misunderstanding and defensiveness, and deep concern about possible retaliation:

  • Expectations felt unclear and misaligned, leading to frustration on both sides.
  • Feedback was experienced as unproductive and, at times, threatening rather than developmental.
  • Each person questioned the other’s intentions, assuming criticism or dismissal where none was intended.
  • Anxiety and risk‑mitigation replaced trust, openness, and shared problem‑solving.

Both individuals named a desire for a relationship that felt safer, clearer, and more predictable. Both hoped to assume good intent, communicate openly, and stay aligned in the midst of constant organizational pressure.

Our Approach

We designed and facilitated a multi‑session 2x2 Coaching engagement, working with dyad both individually and together. The work centered on building awareness, interrupting unhelpful patterns, and establishing new relational norms.

Key elements of our approach included:

  • Surfacing goals and tensions openly. Each person articulated what they needed to feel successful, supported, and secure in the relationship.
  • Building shared language for repair. WBT introduced a Cycle for Repairing Harm, giving the pair a concrete, repeatable process for naming impact, offering genuine apologies, listening without defensiveness, and making amends.
  • Shifting from blame to accountability. Through coaching and reflection, both individuals examined their own patterns around feedback, how power and hierarchy were experienced, and how fear was shaping behavior.
  • Practicing productive conversations. Using advocacy and inquiry, the pair practiced clearer feedback practices, more inquiry, and making explicit requests.
  • Making small, visible commitments. Rather than sweeping promises, the work emphasized small acts of repair and communication practices that could be repeated over time.

Throughout the engagement, we held space for emotion while anchoring the work in practical leadership behaviors. Consistent with the Cape Cod approach, we supported both individuals in slowing down, noticing their patterns in real time, and experimenting with new communication approaches in an effort to help them stay connected to growth.

Outcomes

After a total of 6 90-minute sessions, the manager and employee reported meaningful shifts:

  • Increased clarity around expectations, priorities, and communication norms.
  • Reduced fear and defensiveness, replaced by greater curiosity and generosity of interpretation.
  • More consistent, predictable feedback routines and norms to support earlier communication when challenges arose.

Importantly, the pair left with tools they could continue to use independently, ensuring the relationship could withstand future stress rather than fracture under it.

Why it Matters

We know that interpersonal conflict between two people can take out an entire team. This case illustrates the power of 2x2 Coaching, informed by the Cape Cod approach, when conflict in a relationship starts to feel insurmountable. By investing time and energy in the relationship that has experienced rupture, organizations can transform tension into trust, and misalignment into shared leadership capacity.

When leaders learn how to repair, not retreat, the whole system becomes more resilient.
