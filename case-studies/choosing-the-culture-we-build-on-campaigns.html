Choosing the Culture We Build on Campaigns

Team and leadership development in a statewide coordinated political campaign.

Client
Service
Team Coaching

The team as the unit of change

Background

A statewide coordinated campaign team is responsible for aligning and executing shared voter contact, organizing, and turnout efforts across all Democratic candidates and races in a state.

Key TakeawayEven under relentless campaign pressure, leaders found that culture is a choice—and that time to reconnect as people, not just roles, is what makes it hold.

In the heat of a statewide coordinated campaign, leaders are expected to perform at a relentless pace, managing people, pressure, and politics. The Democratic National Committee partnered with us to pilot leadership development programming with the Virginia Coordinated Campaign, focused on Regional Organizing Directors (RODs) and Deputy Organizing Directors (DODs).

The goal was twofold: to support these leaders as people and managers during an intense campaign cycle, and to gather learnings that could inform future leadership and team development efforts offered to future campaign teams.

The Challenge

in a high-stress, rapidly scaling environment. It’s familiar to anyone who has worked on a campaign:

  • Middle managers were carrying significant emotional and operational load.
  • Team culture was being shaped in real time, often under extreme pressure.
  • The team wanted space to reflect, connect, and build resilience. Timing and system-level constraints complicated the waters.
  • There was a real risk that insight would not translate into sustained behavior change without intentional follow-through.

The question was not whether leadership support was needed - it was how to design and offer it in a way that truly met the moment.

Our Approach

Empower Project’s leadership team faced the dual challenges of intense demands and the emotional toll of managing quickly constructed teams that are built to last only through the cycle. Recognizing the need for structured support, Amanda sought coaching to help leaders manage stress, improve communication, and maintain focus on their mission. We designed and facilitated a multi-modal leadership development pilot that combined retreats, virtual learning, and coaching-informed practices.

Key components included:

  • Leadership Retreats
    A virtual DOD retreat and an in-person regional retreat focused on emotional intelligence, stress management, team culture, and actionable commitments. These sessions emphasized connection, shared language, and reflection. We worked to create rare space for leaders to step out of constant execution mode.
  • Team Culture & Resilience Virtual Workshops
    Leaders explored concepts such as Circles of Control and Influence, managing up and across, and the impact of stress on leadership behavior. These sessions were grounded in real campaign dynamics rather than abstract theory.
  • Iterative Design & Deep Listening
    We adapted session flow and content in real time based on what we learned from participants along the way, spending more time where folks explicitly asked for support (notably around stress, joy, and self-management).
  • Learning-Oriented Partnership
    Throughout the engagement, we gathered feedback, conducted after-action reviews, and surfaced candid observations and recommendations for the DNC and campaign leadership - treating the pilot as both an intervention and a learning lab.

What Shifted

The most powerful impact came from the in-person experiences, after which the team reported:

  • Stronger relationships and a deeper sense of alignment across the team.
  • Increased awareness of how personal stress affects team culture.
  • Renewed commitment to centering joy, assuming positive intent, and supporting one another more intentionally.
  • Greater clarity about individual growth edges as managers and leaders.

The team consistently named connection, emotional resilience, and culture visioning as the most valuable elements of the work. Leaders articulated specific commitments around delegation, communication, self-care, and peer support.

At the same time, the engagement surfaced important constraints:

  • Virtual sessions were less effective given campaign timing and unaddressed system-level pressures.
  • Leaders wanted more role-specific spaces (e.g., ROD-only).

Testimonials

Participants described the engagement as a rare and meaningful pause in an otherwise relentless campaign cycle. They noted that, particularly for in-person time, it offered space to reflect, reconnect, and lead more intentionally.

I learned how much my stress impacts team culture, and that I have real choices about how I show up every day.Campaign Leader

Leaders consistently named increased self-awareness and responsibility for culture as key takeaways:

This created space to connect as people, not just as roles. It reminded me that we choose the culture we build, even under pressure.Regional Leader

Many participants highlighted practical leadership shifts alongside deeper personal insight:

I’m committed to centering joy, assuming positive intent, and being more thoughtful about how I communicate, especially when things are hard.
I realized I need to delegate more, ask for what I need, and seek counsel from my peers instead of carrying everything alone.

Connection and alignment across the team emerged as a core outcome of the in-person experience:

The team cares deeply about each other and this work. Being together in person helped that come back into focus.

Together, these reflections point to the core impact of the work: leaders benefit from time to develop their own self-awareness, connectedness, and intention to building resonant, impactful culture.

I walked out of the session feeling impressed about my new organization's investment in team effectiveness and positive team dynamics. Understanding each other's strengths, motivations, and blind spots is going to be so much easier now, which will unlock our ability to collaborate with understanding, patience, and pride in our teammates and ourselves. Had we not done this retreat, we might have been in danger of failing to see how each of us can fit into and contribute to our team's goals.
